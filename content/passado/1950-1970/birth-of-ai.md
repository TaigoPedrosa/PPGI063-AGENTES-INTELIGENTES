# The Birth of AI and Rationality

> Summary of the first thread of [Agentes Inteligentes no Passado (1950–1970)](pdfs/agentes-inteligentes-1950-1970.pdf).

Before AI was a discipline, three ideas set its foundations: a **mathematical model of
the neuron**, a **behavioral test for intelligence**, and the **conference that gave the
field its name**.

---

## A Logical Calculus of the Ideas Immanent in Nervous Activity (McCulloch & Pitts, 1943)

The first mathematical model of a neuron.

- Took **binary inputs** and used a configurable **threshold** to decide when the neuron
  should fire, based on the weighted sum of its inputs.
- Proved that a network of simple neurons could compute **logical functions** (AND, OR,
  NOT).
- **Weights and threshold were fixed** — these neurons did **not learn**.

> Significance: it showed that reasoning could be expressed as computation over a network
> of simple units, planting the seed for both symbolic logic and, much later, neural
> networks.

---

## The Turing Test (Turing, 1950)

A **practical test** for deciding whether a machine could be considered intelligent.

- If a machine could pass for a human in a **text-based conversation**, and a judge could
  not reliably tell which was which, the machine would be deemed intelligent.
- This is the view of an agent that **simulates human behavior**: the focus is on the
  **output**, not on the internal process of thought.

> Significance: it reframed "can machines think?" into an operational, testable question
> about observable behavior.

---

## The Dartmouth Conference (1956)

The event where the term **Artificial Intelligence** was coined.

- Proposed a working definition of the **thinking machine**: *every aspect of learning or
  any other feature of intelligence can, in principle, be described so precisely that a
  machine can be built to simulate it.*
- Shifted the goal from simple **automation** toward building systems that could **solve
  problems, recognize patterns, and use language**.
- Marked the **birth of symbolic AI**.

---

## Takeaways

- Intelligence was first approached from two angles: **modeling the brain** (McCulloch &
  Pitts) and **modeling behavior** (Turing).
- Dartmouth consolidated these threads into a named research program with an ambitious,
  explicitly stated hypothesis.
- The dominant bet of the era became **symbolic reasoning**, the focus of the next thread.
